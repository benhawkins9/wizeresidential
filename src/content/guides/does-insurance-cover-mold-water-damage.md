I get asked this question a lot, especially after a storm comes through or a homeowner finds mold behind a wall. I want to give you an honest answer — not insurance-sales language, not false reassurance. I'm a handyman, not an adjuster, so take this as general information and always verify with your own insurance agent.

## The Basic Rule: Sudden vs. Gradual

Homeowners insurance is built around sudden, accidental events. A pipe bursts and soaks your drywall overnight — that's the kind of event most policies are written to cover. The water damage and any resulting mold may be covered, subject to your deductible and policy terms.

What most standard policies won't cover: damage that built up over time. A slow drip under a sink that went unnoticed for months, condensation from a poorly ventilated crawl space, mold that developed because an exhaust fan wasn't working. Insurers call this "maintenance" — the position is that you had the opportunity to catch and fix it.

On the Gulf Coast, this distinction matters a lot. Our humidity is unforgiving, and mold can establish itself faster here than in drier climates. If moisture problems go unaddressed, what started as a covered event can turn into a disputed claim.

## What's Usually Covered

- **Sudden pipe burst or appliance failure** that causes water damage and subsequent mold
- **Storm-driven water intrusion** (wind-driven rain through a damaged roof, for example) — though this depends heavily on how your policy defines the covered peril
- **Accidental overflow** from a tub or toilet

Even in these cases, your policy likely has a **mold sub-limit** — a cap on what it will pay for mold remediation specifically. That number can be much lower than the actual remediation cost, especially if the problem has spread.

## What's Usually Not Covered

- Gradual leaks, slow seepage, or moisture buildup over time
- Mold from chronic high humidity or poor ventilation
- Water damage from **flooding** — this requires a separate flood policy
- Damage resulting from deferred maintenance (a roof that needed replacing, a known gutter problem)
- Mold in a property that was left vacant or unoccupied

## Flood Is Different — and Common on the Gulf Coast

If your home took on water from a storm surge, overflowing drainage, or ground saturation, that's flood damage. Standard homeowners policies don't cover it. Flood coverage comes from a separate policy — most commonly through FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program. If you're in a designated flood zone in Jackson County or along the coast and you don't have flood insurance, this is worth a conversation with your agent.
