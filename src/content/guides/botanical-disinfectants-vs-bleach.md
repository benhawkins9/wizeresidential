People ask me why I don't just use bleach for mold. It's a fair question — bleach is cheap, it's everywhere, and it looks like it works. But looking clean and being clean are two different things, especially when it comes to mold in a Gulf Coast home.

## The Problem With Bleach on Porous Surfaces

Bleach is a strong surface disinfectant. On non-porous materials — ceramic tile, glass, a fiberglass shower pan — it does a reasonable job. The problem is that most of the surfaces mold colonizes in a home are porous: drywall, wood studs, OSB sheathing, grout lines.

On porous materials, the water in bleach is absorbed into the surface while the active ingredient (sodium hypochlorite) stays on top and evaporates. The visible mold dies. The mold root structure — the hyphae — stays alive inside the material and keeps growing. A few weeks later, the spot comes back. Sometimes worse.

## What Botanical and AHP Disinfectants Do Differently

EPA-registered botanical disinfectants use plant-derived active ingredients that are formulated to penetrate porous surfaces rather than sit on top of them. Accelerated hydrogen peroxide (AHP) products work similarly — they break down into water and oxygen after doing their job, leaving no chemical residue and no harsh fumes.

For occupied homes in Ocean Springs and across the Gulf Coast, this matters. Bleach fumes can irritate airways, especially for kids, elderly residents, or anyone with asthma. I work in a lot of homes where people live day-to-day, and I'm not going to flood the space with caustic chemicals when a safer, more effective option exists.

## Containment Still Matters

No disinfectant — botanical or otherwise — replaces proper containment and source control. When I take on a [mold remediation](/services/mold-remediation) job, the first priority is identifying where the moisture is coming from. A bathroom exhaust fan that vents into the attic instead of outside. A slow drip under a sink that soaked the cabinet base for months. A crawlspace with no vapor barrier.

Treat the mold without fixing the source and it comes right back. Every time.

## A Note on DIY Mold Treatment

Small surface spots — less than a few square feet on a non-porous surface — are generally manageable for a homeowner. Anything larger, anything involving HVAC systems, or any mold that followed water damage or a storm should get a professional look. The risk of spreading spores during treatment is real, and the health implications are serious enough to take seriously.
