Hurricane season on the MS Gulf Coast is not a maybe — it's a when. Living in Ocean Springs, you already know that. What I've seen over the years, though, is that most storm damage isn't caused by catastrophic wind alone. It's caused by small vulnerabilities that let water in, and water does the real damage from there.

Here are five handyman tasks that make a genuine difference before a storm arrives.

## 1. Inspect Doors and Windows — Especially Weatherstripping

Your doors and windows are the first line of defense against wind-driven rain. Old or damaged weatherstripping allows water to work its way inside even in a moderate storm, let alone a hurricane. Check every exterior door for gaps, compression seal condition, and threshold fit. Look at window frames for cracked caulk or failed glazing compound.

[Door and window repairs](/services/door-window) are some of the most straightforward pre-season jobs, and they also help your energy bills all summer long. Don't put them off.

## 2. Clear and Secure Your Gutters

Clogged gutters during heavy rain cause overflow that saturates your fascia, soffit, and the soil directly against your foundation. Before storm season, gutters should be clean, properly sloped toward downspouts, and firmly attached to the house. If your gutters are pulling away from the fascia, that's a sign the board behind them may already be deteriorating — which brings us to the next item.

## 3. Check Fascia and Soffit for Rot or Loose Sections

Fascia and soffit that are soft, cracked, or only loosely attached won't hold up under sustained wind and rain. This is one of the most overlooked parts of hurricane prep on the Gulf Coast. Wind gets under loose soffit panels, and once that happens, you can lose significant sections of your overhang in a storm.

If there's any [wood rot present](/services/carpentry-wood-rot), it needs to be cut out and replaced properly — not just painted or caulked over. Loose panels need to be re-fastened with appropriate exterior fasteners, not just a couple of screws pushed back in.

## 4. Seal Water Intrusion Points

Walk around your home and look for any place where different materials meet: where siding meets trim, where pipes or conduit penetrate the wall, where the chimney (if you have one) meets the roofline. These transitions are where caulk ages and fails first. A tube of paintable exterior caulk and an hour of work can prevent significant water intrusion during a storm.

Pay particular attention to the area above windows and doors. Failed head flashing or dried-out caulk above a window frame is a common source of interior wall water damage after a Gulf Coast storm.

## 5. Think About Your Water After the Storm

This one doesn't prevent storm damage, but it affects your recovery. After major hurricanes, municipal water advisories are common, and private wells can be contaminated by flooding and debris infiltration. Having a working whole-home or under-sink filtration system means you have clean water available during the cleanup period when you need it most.

It's worth thinking about before storm season rather than after — contractors, plumbers, and supply chains all get stretched thin once a storm passes through.
