If you've lived on the Mississippi Gulf Coast for any length of time, you know the air here is different. It's heavy, salty, and rarely truly dry. That combination is hard on a lot of things — your hair, your car, and especially the wood on your house. Wood rot is one of the most common repair calls I get in Ocean Springs and throughout Jackson County, and it almost always costs more than it would have if it had been caught earlier.

Here's what's actually happening, how to find it before it spreads, and what a proper repair looks like.

## Why the Gulf Coast Accelerates Wood Decay

Wood rot is caused by fungi — microscopic organisms that digest wood fibers. Like most living things, they need moisture, oxygen, and a food source (the wood itself) to thrive. What they need most is moisture content above about 20 percent in the wood.

On the Gulf Coast, we hand them everything they need on a platter:

- **Relative humidity** regularly runs above 80 percent, even when it's not raining.
- **Salt air** draws moisture into wood and accelerates the breakdown of protective coatings.
- **Heat** speeds up fungal metabolism, so rot that might take years to develop in a drier climate can establish itself in a single wet season here.

Fascia boards (the horizontal trim behind your gutters), soffits (the underside of your roof overhang), deck framing, and exterior window and door trim are the usual first victims. These areas are constantly exposed to weather, often hold moisture from roof runoff, and don't always get the attention they deserve.

## How to Spot It Early

Walk around your house and look for these warning signs:

- Paint that peels or blisters in the same spot year after year
- Discoloration — gray, orange, or black staining on wood surfaces
- Soft or spongy texture when you press on the wood with a screwdriver or your thumb
- Crumbling at the corners or end-grain of boards
- Gutters pulling away from fascia — often means the board behind them is compromised

End-grain exposure is especially vulnerable. Any cut end of a board that's exposed to rain without proper sealing will absorb water rapidly. That's why the corners of window casings and the tops of porch posts are common starting points for decay.

## What a Proper Wood Rot Repair MS Actually Involves

Painting over rot or caulking around it doesn't fix it. Here's what I do:

1. **Remove all rotted material.** Soft, compromised wood has to come out. Leaving it in contact with new wood just gives the fungi a place to continue growing.
2. **Find and address the moisture source.** A rotted fascia usually means a gutter is clogged, mis-sloped, or has a hole. A rotted window sill often means the flashing or caulk above it failed. The rot is a symptom — the water intrusion is the problem.
3. **Apply borate treatment** to any adjacent wood that shows early signs of fungi or that will be in contact with the repair.
4. **Prime all six sides of replacement lumber** before installation — top, bottom, both faces, and both cut ends.
5. **Seal all joints and penetrations** with a paintable exterior caulk rated for the application.

Skipping any of these steps means you're likely replacing the same board again in a few years.

## Don't Let It Reach the Framing

Fascia and trim rot is manageable. When it gets into the structural framing — roof rafters, wall studs, floor joists — you're looking at a much more involved and expensive repair. The sooner you catch it, the better.
