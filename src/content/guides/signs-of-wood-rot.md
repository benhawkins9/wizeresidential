Living on the Mississippi Gulf Coast means dealing with heat, humidity, and rain that most of the country never sees. That combination is wood rot's best friend. I've repaired rotted fascia, deck posts, and door frames on homes all over Ocean Springs and Jackson County — and in almost every case, catching it earlier would have cost the homeowner a lot less money.

Here are seven signs you should know how to spot.

## 1. Soft or Spongy Wood

This is the most reliable test. Press your thumb firmly against any wood trim, post, or board that looks questionable. Healthy wood doesn't budge. Rotted wood feels soft, compresses under pressure, or crumbles. A flathead screwdriver works even better — if it sinks in without much force, rot is present.

## 2. Bubbling, Peeling, or Flaking Paint

Paint doesn't bubble on dry wood. When you see paint lifting on siding, trim, or fascia, moisture is trapped underneath. That moisture is exactly what dry rot and wet rot need to take hold. Don't just repaint — pull back the paint and check the wood beneath.

## 3. Musty or Earthy Smell Near Woodwork

Fungal decay has a distinct smell, somewhere between wet soil and a damp basement. If you notice it near a window frame, under a deck, or at the base of a porch post, trust your nose. Rot is often further along than what's visible on the surface.

## 4. Discolored or Darkened Wood

Healthy painted or stained wood holds its color. Rotted wood often turns gray, brown, or black — especially along the grain. On unpainted wood like deck boards or fence posts, look for dark streaking or patches that stand out from the surrounding grain.

## 5. Flexing Boards or Wobbly Railings

A deck board that bounces underfoot, a railing post that wobbles when grabbed, or a step that feels soft — these are signs that rot has eaten into the structural fibers, not just the surface. This is where it stops being cosmetic and starts being a safety issue.

## 6. Crumbling or Stringy Wood Texture

Two main types of rot present differently: brown rot causes wood to crack into cube-like pieces and crumble when touched; white rot leaves behind a stringy, spongy mass that's lighter in color. Either way, the wood has lost its structural integrity and needs to come out.

## 7. Where to Look First on Coastal Homes

Rot follows water. On Gulf Coast homes, the highest-risk areas are:

- **Fascia and soffits** — where gutters hold standing water or overflow
- **Eaves and overhangs** — exposed to driving rain from storms
- **Deck ledger boards and ground-contact posts** — constant moisture from soil and splash-back
- **Window and door frames** — especially on the west and south sides that take the most weather
- **Any wood within six inches of the ground**

## Rot vs. Termites — and When It's Structural

Rot and termite damage can look alike, but rot clusters around moisture sources while termites follow the grain and leave mud tubes. If you suspect both, it's worth having both inspected at the same time.

As for structural concern: surface rot on trim or siding is usually repairable with epoxy consolidant and filler, or a straightforward board replacement. When rot reaches rim joists, subflooring, structural posts, or load-bearing beams, you're in structural territory and the repair scope grows significantly.

The honest answer is the sooner you catch it, the better. A single rotted fascia board is a modest repair. Rot that's spread into the roof decking or a deck frame is a much bigger project.
