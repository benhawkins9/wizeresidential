Ocean Springs has some genuinely beautiful older homes — bungalows, craftsman cottages, mid-century houses with good bones and character that newer construction doesn't replicate. They're also homes that demand a different approach to maintenance and repair than a subdivision house built in the last 20 years.

I've worked on older coastal homes throughout the area, and the consistent lesson is this: sympathetic repairs that respect original materials last longer and look better than quick substitutions that ignore what was already there.

## Wood Rot: Save Before You Replace

Wood rot is the most common structural issue in older Gulf Coast homes. Fascia boards, window sills, exterior trim, porch posts — anywhere wood is exposed to the persistent humidity and occasional flooding of the Mississippi Gulf Coast is a candidate.

The default response is to rip it out and replace it with new lumber or a PVC substitute. Sometimes that's the right call. But on a historic home, the original trim profiles, wood species, and scale are part of what makes the home look right. Replacement lumber — especially modern pine, which is less rot-resistant than older-growth material — often doesn't match visually and may not hold up as well as the remaining original wood around it.

A better first step is assessment: how deep does the rot go? Shallow surface rot on an otherwise sound board can often be stabilized with a penetrating epoxy consolidant, filled with epoxy filler, and painted back to match. The repair is invisible and the original material is preserved. I handle [carpentry and wood rot repair](/services/carpentry-wood-rot) across Ocean Springs and the MS Gulf Coast with this approach in mind.

## Original Doors and Windows

The doors and windows in older homes are worth preserving when possible. Pre-1960s wood windows were often made from old-growth fir or pine with tight grain that is genuinely more durable than what's available today. The hardware is often repairable. The sash can be reglazed. Weatherstripping can be added.

Replacement windows in a historic home frequently look wrong — modern profiles don't match the originals, and the visual result undermines the character of the house. Before I recommend replacement, I look at adjustment, repair, and weatherproofing. [Door and window](/services/door-window) issues in older homes are usually solvable without full replacement.

## Plaster vs. Drywall

Homes built before the 1950s typically have plaster walls. Plaster is harder and more durable than drywall in many ways, but it cracks with foundation movement and is more demanding to repair well. Slapping a drywall patch over a plaster repair shows. The texture, the sound, and the finished appearance all differ.

On historic homes I match the repair method to the wall material. Plaster repairs use plaster; drywall repairs use drywall compound. If original plaster is too damaged to repair, full replacement to drywall is sometimes the right decision, but that's a scope conversation, not a default.

## Moisture Control Is Non-Negotiable

Older construction doesn't have the vapor barriers, house wraps, and tight building envelopes that modern homes do. That means moisture — from Gulf Coast humidity, from storm events, from condensation — has more paths into the structure.

Crawlspace vapor barriers, adequate soffit and ridge ventilation, and prompt attention to any penetration in the roof or siding are the foundation of long-term care for a historic home. Letting moisture accumulate is how you end up with the wood rot, mold, and structural damage that makes a beautiful old house expensive to bring back.

A consistent maintenance routine — checking the crawlspace annually, inspecting flashing after major storms, repainting exterior wood before it starts to fail — is genuinely less expensive than the remediation work that follows neglect.
